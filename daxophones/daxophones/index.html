Hand crafted daxophones and additional tongues.
 
The daxophone is based on the principle of bowing thin pieces of wood called tongues. Each tongue derives its acoustic properties from its density, grain, and shape. The body of the daxophone involves a clamp to hold the tongue and a thin, top sound board attached to two contact microphones on its underside. The mics send their signal out a quarter-inch jack.
 
The daxophone is played by bowing (or tapping, scraping, plucking) a wooden tongue. The effective length of vibration of the tongue can be shortened by moving the dax across it. The dax is a tear drop shaped piece of heavy wood.
 
The daxophone was originally designed by Hans Reichel. While the daxophone can come in many shapes, my daxophones are based upon Hans' instructions found on his exceptional website which has a lot of other information about the daxophone.

Narrowing Strip with a Hole

Shape
Hans Reichel made many tongues of this shape. This family features a very nice sweep of tones from low to high, with a break at the hole. It also features a wide bowing area (upper right) that allows you to change the brightness of your sound, depending on the width of the bowed area.

Wood Type
Wood types like lacewood have very straight grains that can add a lot of rasp to your tone. The sound is buzzier, great for when you get bored with the purity of dense woods like rosewood. These straight grains make shaping harder. They don't work well for delicate narrow shapes.
 


 

The Long Gap

Shape
Tongues with a long hole in the middle might attempt to resonate at two tones. They exhibit a vocal style formant that changes with the pitch. This particular tongue has a wide bowing area providing a lot of variety in tone. Bowing the narrow strip at the hole will immediately raise the pitch, like squeaking on a clarinet. Gentle playing might result in quiet flute-like tones.

Wood Type
Rosewood and other very dense hardwoods that exhibit strength both with and across the grain can be shaped into much more delicate shapes. Holes and narrow strips are less likely to snap. They are a bit harder to bow, but have a much clearer, centered tone.


 

Rectangular Slot

Shape
The slotted rectangle family of tongues are very nice for playing melodically. While the daxophone is a very difficult instrument to find specific pitches, ruler shaped tongues at the very least don't skip over notes in a scale like many of the wilder shapes will. You might get a usable two octaves out of a tongue with this shape. Length determines which octaves. Unlike most tongues which are bowed along the far edge, these are best bowed along the rightmost edge. Bowing along the narrow strip along the top is less controlled and can produce quiet flute-like tones and loud screeching.
 

Wood Type
Maple has a nice sound, fairly focused but also with a different timbre from exotic hardwoods. It feels scrubbier to me and has a very different set of formants.


 

Multi-hole Wedge

Shape
A sharp wedge shape gives a tongue a nice range. The holes here create skips in the playable notes of the tongue. The effect is similar to the way the frets on the dax work creating yodel-type jumps from one note to another. The nonlinearity makes finding new sounds playful.
 



 

Multi-prong Tongues

Shape
Prongs provide limited range, but fairly good pitch control when they are thick. Multiple prongs provide timbral variety. If they are spaced correctly, you can put a bow in between them and get some great fiddling effects. Other possibilties include prongs of different lengths or placing a whole where they meet to increase the variability of timbre.
